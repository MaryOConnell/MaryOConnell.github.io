Gestalt Theory In Design

Overview

Gestalt (whole form) psychology is a theory of mind that originated in the Berlin School in the 1920s. It describes the laws of acquiring and maintaining meaningful perceptions from an apparently chaotic world. The central principle is that the human mind has self-organising tendencies. The viewer perceives interactions between various visual stimuli and interprets them as a whole object, independent of the parts.

Gestalt rules have been incorporated into design theory as a means to effectively communicate ideas and information between the designer and the recipient. This website presents this theory with examples of how it has been applied to various types of design.

Gestalt Principles In Architectural Design

The principles of Gestalt are apparent in all architectural design, both historic and modern. Ancient architecture is an example of how people attempted to assign order to their world. Examination of enduring ancient structures such as Mayan cities, the Roman Colosseum and Egyptian temples reveal how our ancestors interpreted and constructed their surroundings.

Modern architecture continues to make use of these Gestalt properties. Examples can be found around the world, from the smallest cottage to some of the world's tallest buildings.

Examples Of Gestalt Theory In Modern Design

The principles of Gestalt are used in the context of modern visual and interactive media. However, early Gestalt theory did not anticipate digital design and newer applications of visual media. The original laws still hold true in modern design. However, the early theory has been expanded upon and rules have been developed further to the original principles to accommodate these applications.
