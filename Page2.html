Law of Proximity

The Law of Proximity indicates that elements that are near to each other tend to be perceived as a single unit. Here, the spaces between the glazing are defined by the stone structure. We interpret the glass as a single element and the walls as another, even though they are both composed of smaller units, i.e., panes of glass and blocks.

Grouping elements that belong together is a way of defining categories. You can easily group the components of one category by placing them near each other.

This law is regularly used as a usability feature, for example, in the control panel of a stereo system or the dashboard of a car. It is also evident in graphic design layouts, such as magazines.

Law of Symmetry

People view symmetry as pleasing and comforting and tend to prefer symmetric appearances over asymmetric ones. Symmetric objects or arrangements are associated with positive aspects such as stability, consistency and structure. Asymmetric arrangements on the other hand give a rather negative impression, like something is wrong, missing, or out of balance.

Much architectural design is based on symmetric grid systems . This is done not only for aesthetic reasons but also for economy of cost and to streamline the design process.

Furniture design is generally symmetric, for example, tables and wardrobes, as well as the shape of foodstuffs packaging, such as egg cartons and cereal boxes.

Law of Similarity

The Law of Similarity states that items that appear similar are perceived as a single element. So elements that have the same shape, colour or other common characteristics, are perceived to be belonging together. This phenomenon can be very useful to group items that belong to one category.

Software applications use similarity to group together icons and menu bar content in a logical way. This allows users to more easily navigate the user interface.

Law of Continuity

The principle of continuity dictates that once the eye begins to follow something, it will continue travelling in that direction until it encounters another object.

Architecture contains many subtle and obvious clues to lead people around a structure, both visually and physically. For example, decorative canopies are sometimes erected over doorways to emphasise the location of an entrance. Handrails on staircases physically lead a person onto a different level of a building.

Other ways to lead the eye include a photograph or illustration that contains an image of a person. The person featured should be looking toward the rest of the design or have their body facing in that direction.

Figure Ground

The Law of Figure and Ground is based on the relationship between an element and its surrounding space. It describes how we more easily perceive a figure than its background. When one element is placed on top of another, an impression of depth emerges.

This principle is also referred to as positive and negative space, the positive being the object and the negative referring to the space around it. In this example, the black cladding elements appear to pop out and the white cladding appears recessed.
